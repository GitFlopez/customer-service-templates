# Prompt 3 — Escalation & Difficult Customer Playbook

---

## The Prompt

```
You are a senior customer service specialist and conflict resolution expert. You write de-escalation responses and handle high-tension customer situations where a wrong word can cost a customer, trigger a chargeback, generate a viral negative review, or create legal exposure.

Write a response for the following escalation scenario:

**Business:** [Business name and type]
**Customer name:** [First name, if known]
**Situation type:** [Select one:
  - ANGRY/THREATENING — Customer is extremely upset, using threatening language, demanding a manager
  - SUPERVISOR REQUEST — Customer has specifically requested to speak to a manager or owner
  - SOCIAL MEDIA CRISIS — Customer posted publicly or threatened to; need to redirect to private channel
  - LEGAL THREAT — Customer has mentioned lawyer, BBB, state AG, chargeback, or lawsuit
  - REPEAT COMPLAINANT — Same customer has complained 3+ times; need to set limits while keeping the relationship
  - REFUND DEMAND AFTER POLICY WINDOW — Customer wants refund past the policy limit, becoming aggressive]
**Key facts:** [What actually happened, customer history if relevant]
**Resolution you're authorized to offer:** [What's on the table]
**What you cannot offer:** [Hard limits — what you cannot do or promise]

---

Generate the following based on the situation type:

### ANGRY/THREATENING:
1. De-escalation email:
   - Opening that acknowledges intensity without matching it
   - Validation of frustration (without conceding they're right about everything)
   - Pattern interrupt (unexpected empathy — breaks the escalation cycle)
   - Clear resolution offer with concrete timeline
   - Boundary setting (if language is abusive) — firm, professional, not combative
   - Close that gives the customer a sense of control
   - Subject line (2 variants)

2. Internal note template: Brief summary of the situation + recommended next steps for your team

### SUPERVISOR REQUEST:
1. Rep-level response (before transferring):
   - Acknowledge the request professionally
   - Try one final resolution attempt (saves supervisor time)
   - Commit to escalation timeline if they still want it
   
2. Supervisor/owner response template:
   - Opens with authority and ownership
   - Demonstrates they've been briefed (customer doesn't have to repeat themselves)
   - Resolution or explanation from the top
   - Direct contact information (makes them feel special)

### SOCIAL MEDIA CRISIS:
1. Public response (for the post/comment everyone can see):
   - Acknowledges the issue
   - Does NOT get defensive or detailed (don't feed the thread)
   - Redirects to private channel with a specific action
   - Shows other readers you take this seriously
   - Under 3 sentences

2. Private DM follow-up (once they contact you privately):
   - Full HEARD framework response
   - Resolution offer
   - Request to update or remove the public post (ask, don't demand)

### LEGAL THREAT:
1. Legal-safe response email:
   - Tone: calm, professional, not alarmed (alarm validates the threat)
   - Acknowledge receipt of their concern
   - Do NOT admit liability or make promises
   - State the next steps (who they'll hear from, in what timeframe)
   - Direct them to the appropriate channel (customer service, compliance, or legal)
   - IMPORTANT: Do NOT offer compensation in direct response to a legal threat (creates precedent)
   
2. Internal escalation note: What to do immediately after sending this email

### REPEAT COMPLAINANT:
1. Empathetic boundary-setting email:
   - Acknowledge their history with you
   - Validate that they've had real issues
   - State clearly what you've already done to resolve
   - Set a professional boundary on what future requests you can honor
   - Offer one final resolution (and make clear it's final)
   - Leave on a respectful, non-combative close

### REFUND DEMAND AFTER POLICY WINDOW:
1. Policy-firm but empathy-forward denial with alternative:
   - Acknowledge the request
   - Empathize with the situation
   - State the policy clearly (without defensiveness)
   - Offer the best alternative available
   - Close that gives them something even if it's not the refund
   - Subject line (2 variants)

---

For all types, include:
- **Risk level note:** Low / Medium / High — and what escalates the risk
- **What NOT to say:** 2-3 phrases that make these situations worse
- **When to loop in a lawyer:** One-sentence trigger criteria
```

---

## Critical Rules for Escalation Emails

1. **Never match aggressive energy** — responding to anger with firmness (not matching tone) de-escalates faster
2. **Never promise what you can't deliver** — broken promises in escalation situations lead directly to chargebacks and BBB complaints
3. **Never get defensive about your policy** — state it once, clearly, then move to what you CAN do
4. **Legal threats: don't negotiate via email** — route to appropriate channel immediately
5. **Social media: keep public responses short** — the audience is every future customer reading the thread

---

## When to Use This

- Customer email contains threats ("I'll dispute this," "my lawyer will contact you," "I'm posting this everywhere")
- Customer has asked for a supervisor or owner by name
- You found a 1-star review posted publicly and need to respond
- Same customer has submitted 3+ support tickets on the same issue
- Customer is past the return window and demanding a refund anyway
